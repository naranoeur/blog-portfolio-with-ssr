What to do ahead of time

Obtain wilderness permit either by mail or in person to camp overnight in the area.

Obtain National Forest Adventure Pass to be able to park overnight.

Obtain campfire permit to use camper's stove.

Learn more at https://www.fs.usda.gov/angeles.

Icehouse Canyon trail was really pretty with all the cedar and oak trees and the creek next to the trail.
Parking

There was a parking lot near the Icehouse Canyon trailhead, however at around 10am, when we got there it was full. Many people parked along the road. We parked along the road 5-minute walk away from the trailhead.

Closer to the Cedar Gleen campground, the trees thinned out revealing the view of the mountains.
Trail

We started at the Icehouse Canyon trailhead. The first two miles were well-shaded and along a creek. The elevation gain was minimal. Then we took a fork off the icehouse canyon trail and hiked another mile to the campsite. The second part of the trail had numerous switchbacks. As we gained elevation, the trees thinned out, and the last stretch we walked in the sun without any shade.

Cedar Glenn campground was well shaded and had many curious blue jays who came to check out what we were doing.
Campground

Cedar Glenn campground had a nice flat area that is not too large with cedar trees. There is a small hill that you can climb with scenic overlook. The city is visible in the distance, and at sunset, the Los Angeles city skyline became visible. At night, the city lights looked beautiful in the distance.

At sunset, Los Angeles city skyline became visible from the campsite.
Water Source

There was a small creek near campsite. It was accessible in two locations from the trail, maybe 5-10 minute walk away from the campsite. Although there were many yellow jackets in the area, it is still possible to get to the water.

The full moon lit up the area. The city lights shimmered in the distance. And in the silence of the forest, I had some of the best sleep I've ever had.
